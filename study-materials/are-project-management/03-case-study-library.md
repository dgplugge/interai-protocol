# Case Study 3: Municipal Library Branch

## Scenario Narrative

CivicSpace Architects, a 10-person firm focused on public and institutional projects, has been selected by the City of Glenbrook to design a new branch library. The Glenbrook East Branch Library includes a single-story building of approximately 9,600 SF on a 0.8-acre municipal lot. The program includes adult collection, children's area, teen zone, two meeting rooms, a maker space, staff workroom, and public restrooms. Site work includes a 28-space parking lot, accessible entry plaza, stormwater rain garden, and book drop lane. The City's capital improvement policy mandates LEED Silver certification (minimum) for all new municipal buildings over 5,000 SF.

Key team members at CivicSpace include Denise Okafor, AIA, LEED AP (Principal-in-Charge), James Thorne (Project Manager — your role for this case study), and Elena Vasquez (Project Architect / Interior Designer). Consultants include Atlas Engineering (structural), ClearPoint MEP (mechanical, electrical, plumbing), SiteLogic Civil (civil engineering and landscape), and EcoMetrics (LEED consulting and energy modeling). The general contractor is Daniels Municipal Construction, selected via competitive sealed bid per city procurement code as the lowest responsible bidder.

The total project budget is $4,800,000, funded by a municipal bond referendum. The construction budget is $3,900,000. Soft costs total $900,000 covering design fees, permits, FF&E, technology, and contingency. Construction contingency is $195,000, representing 5% of the construction budget. Per city policy, any change order exceeding $25,000 requires City Council approval at a public meeting. Council meetings are held the first and third Tuesday of each month.

The project timeline is as follows: design started September 2, 2024. SD completion was November 15, 2024. DD completion was February 14, 2025. CD completion was May 16, 2025. Public bid opening occurred June 24, 2025, with contract award on July 8, 2025 and construction start on August 4, 2025. Substantial completion is targeted for May 22, 2026, with a grand opening target of July 4, 2026 coinciding with the City's 150th anniversary celebration. Total construction duration is 42 weeks.

It is currently November 2025, approximately 13 weeks into construction. The project has been proceeding on schedule, but several issues have emerged. First, during bidding the low bid came in at $4,180,000, which was $280,000 over budget. A value engineering package was negotiated to reduce the contract to $3,920,000, only $20,000 over budget, absorbed from contingency. Some VE substitutions are now causing quality and compliance concerns. Second, the contractor has submitted 40 of 68 required submittals, but 18 are still pending architect review. The contractor claims the backlog is delaying procurement and will cause schedule delays. Third, the Library Director has requested adding a drive-through book return lane with an automated sorting system, which was removed during VE. The Library Board has since allocated $85,000 from their operating budget for this feature. Fourth, the LEED consultant has flagged that two VE substitutions may not meet EPD requirements for LEED Materials and Resources credits, potentially dropping the project below the Silver threshold.

Value Engineering Log — Negotiated Reductions with Daniels Municipal Construction:

VE-01: Roofing membrane changed from 80-mil TPO, Class A rated, EPD-certified to 60-mil TPO, Class A rated, no EPD. Savings $34,000.
VE-02: Interior paint changed from low-VOC with HPD and EPD certification to low-VOC with HPD only, no EPD. Savings $8,000.
VE-03: Meeting room operable partition changed from STC-52 rated to STC-48 rated. Savings $12,000.
VE-04: Parking lot paving changed from full-depth asphalt (4-inch base plus 2-inch surface) to reduced section (3-inch base plus 1.5-inch surface). Savings $18,000.
VE-05: Automated book return lane with drive-through return and sorting system eliminated entirely. Savings $85,000.
VE-06: Storefront glazing system changed from thermally broken aluminum with triple-pane to thermally broken aluminum with dual-pane. Savings $42,000.
VE-07: Ceiling tile changed from mineral fiber NRC 0.90 with recycled content to mineral fiber NRC 0.85 standard. Savings $11,000.
VE-08: Site concrete walks changed from integral color with exposed aggregate to standard broom finish. Savings $16,000.
VE-09: Landscape plantings changed from native species per LEED SS credit to reduced palette, still native-compliant. Savings $14,000.
VE-10: Earthwork changed from exporting 400 CY excess soil to spreading on-site and regrading. Savings $20,000.

Total VE savings: $260,000. Revised contract sum: $3,920,000. Over budget by $20,000, absorbed from contingency, reducing contingency to $175,000.

EcoMetrics Memo — November 5, 2025

TO: James Thorne, CivicSpace Architects. FROM: Nadia Kowalski, EcoMetrics. RE: LEED Materials and Resources Credit Risk — VE Items 01 and 02.

James — During our LEED documentation review, we identified that two value engineering substitutions are jeopardizing 4 points in the Materials and Resources category.

MR Credit: Building Product Disclosure and Optimization — EPD (2 points). This credit requires a minimum of 20 permanently installed products with Environmental Product Declarations. The original design specified 23 products with EPDs. VE-01 (roofing membrane) and VE-02 (interior paint) both removed EPD-certified products. Current count is 21 EPD-certified products. However, Submittals 07-01 and 09-01 were returned revise and resubmit because the submitted products lack EPDs. If the contractor installs the non-EPD products per the VE specification, the count drops to 19, below the 20-product threshold.

MR Credit: Building Product Disclosure and Optimization — Material Ingredients (2 points). This requires Health Product Declarations for 20 products. VE-02 (paint) retained HPD-only certification. This credit is currently at 20 products with no margin for error.

Options: (1) Revert VE-01 and VE-02 to original EPD-certified products at an additional cost of $42,000. (2) Find alternative EPD-certified products at similar cost to the VE substitutes, requiring contractor cooperation and resubmittal. (3) Accept the point loss and attempt to make up 4 points elsewhere, which is difficult at this stage since most other credits are already maximized or require design changes.

Recommendation: Option 2 is preferred. Two potential substitute products have been identified. For roofing, GenFlex EZ TPO 60-mil is EPD-certified with a cost delta of approximately $8,000 versus the VE substitute. For paint, Sherwin-Williams Harmony is EPD and HPD certified with a cost delta of approximately $3,000 versus the VE substitute. Total additional cost for Option 2 is approximately $11,000, significantly less than reverting to original specs.

This needs to be resolved before the products are ordered. The roofing membrane has a 4-week lead time and is scheduled for installation starting January 12, 2026. Please advise.

— Nadia
