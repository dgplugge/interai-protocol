## Case Study 1: Riverfront Mixed-Use Development

### Question 1 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** CO / M

The green roof is a change to the Owner's program after SD approval. Document A, § 3.2
explicitly designates "services necessitated by changes in the Owner's program or budget
after SD approval" as Additional Services compensated at hourly rates per Exhibit B.
Marcus must issue a fee proposal and obtain written authorization before beginning any
design work on the green roof.

**Why not A?** The green roof is new scope, not part of CA basic services. CA covers
administration of the *existing* design, not new design work.

**Why not C?** Although sustainability consulting is listed in § 3.2, it was explicitly
"Not Designated" — meaning it is not part of Basic Services. Green roof design is new
scope regardless.

**Why not D?** Design services are professional fees, not reimbursable expenses. § 5.3
covers printing, travel, and similar costs.

**Cross-reference:** Document A § 3.2, § 5.2 (Exhibit B rates)

---

### Question 2 — Answer: **C**
**Tag:** RK / H

The geotechnical investigation was an Owner-furnished service (§ 4.3). That investigation
failed to identify the contamination. StructurePro's original fixed fee was scoped based
on the information available — the redesign from spread footings to a mat foundation was
caused by an unforeseen condition that originated from inadequate Owner-furnished
information. The Architect should help StructurePro document the claim and present it to
the Owner.

**Why not A?** Fixed-fee contracts have defined scopes. Redesign for conditions not
identified in the Owner's geotechnical report is outside that scope.

**Why not B?** Meridian coordinates consultants (§ 2.1), but coordination does not mean
absorbing their costs for Owner-caused scope changes. § 3.3 specifically provides for
additional compensation when circumstances beyond the Architect's control require extra
services.

**Why not D?** The contractual relationship is Architect-to-Consultant and
Architect-to-Owner. StructurePro doesn't have standing to file directly against the
Owner's geotechnical engineer.

**Cross-reference:** Document A §§ 2.1, 3.3, 4.3; Document C Email 4

### Question 3 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** PE / M

Document B's schedule note under PCO-007 states: "0-2 weeks if approved before Jul 15"
vs. "3-5 weeks if approved after Aug 1." July 15 is the threshold that minimizes schedule
impact given the existing 2-week delay.

**Why not A?** June 23 is the meeting date Marcus proposed, not a design/approval
deadline.

**Why not C?** After August 1, the impact increases to 3-5 weeks.

**Why not D?** September 1 is the stormwater ordinance date, not a green roof decision
deadline.

**Cross-reference:** Document B, PCO-007 notes

### Question 4 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** CO / H

The claim has two components. The $18,000 idle crew claim has merit (crew mobilized
during remediation delay) but needs verification. The $29,000 material escalation is
undocumented — per A201 § 15.1.4, claims must be supported with substantiating data.
The Architect's role is to review documentation and certify only what is substantiated.

**Why not A?** Certifying the full $47,000 without reviewing documentation violates the
Architect's duty of independent review.

**Why not C?** While the subcontractor's contract is with the GC, the Architect reviews
and certifies change orders for the Owner. The soil remediation was an Owner-risk event,
so legitimate delay costs flow through.

**Why not D?** Section 2.5 relates to construction means/methods/safety — not to the
Architect's role in reviewing claims and certifying payment.

**Cross-reference:** Document C Emails 3; AIA A201 § 15.1.4; Document A § 2.5

### Question 5 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** WP / M

The critical path runs through Exterior Enclosure and MEP Rough-In — both have zero
float. Overlapping L4-6 enclosure and adding a second MEP crew targets recovery on
critical-path activities. Activities with float (Interior Finishes, Rooftop Amenity)
cannot recover schedule because they don't control the completion date.

**Why not A?** Interior Finishes have float, meaning accelerating them doesn't recover
the critical path.

**Why not C?** 1 week of float on the Rooftop Amenity doesn't address the 2-week delay,
and it's not on the critical path.

**Why not D?** Deferring punchlist doesn't eliminate the delay — it just shifts the
problem and risks occupancy issues.

**Cross-reference:** Document B, Critical Path and Float Analysis

### Question 6 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** RK / H

When a permitted project submits modifications after a new ordinance takes effect, the
modification itself typically triggers compliance review under the new requirements. The
green roof would be a site modification involving stormwater characteristics. Only the
modification scope would likely need to comply — not the entire project — but it could
still require additional civil engineering and potentially help with compliance if the
green roof reduces stormwater runoff.

**Why not A?** The existing permitted project is grandfathered; only modifications trigger
new compliance.

**Why not C?** Grandfathering applies to the *original* permit, not to post-permit
modifications.

**Why not D?** Most jurisdictions apply new ordinances to modifications of permitted
projects, not only to entirely new projects.

**Cross-reference:** Document C Email 5; general regulatory knowledge

### Question 7 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** RM / M

Additional Services must be authorized by the Owner before the Architect directs
consultants to begin work. Both Meridian's fee and StructurePro's fee for the green roof
review need Owner approval. Beginning work without authorization creates a financial risk
for the firm.

**Why not A?** Starting work and negotiating later exposes the firm to unpaid work if the
Owner ultimately declines the green roof.

**Why not C?** Asking consultants to work for free is unprofessional and unsustainable.

**Why not D?** Structural analysis is outside the Architect's professional competency.
The Project Architect is not a licensed structural engineer.

**Cross-reference:** Document A §§ 3.1, 3.2, 5.2; Document C Email 4

### Question 8 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** PE / E

Document A § 11.1 explicitly states: if the CA phase exceeds 14 months through no fault
of the Architect, compensation adjusts to hourly rates per Exhibit B for the overage
period. CA started January 6, 2025 — 14 months = March 6, 2026. The current projected
completion (March 13) already exceeds this by one week, and the green roof could extend
it further.

**Why not A?** The trigger is 14 months, not 60 weeks, and only if the extension is not
the Architect's fault.

**Why not C?** § 11.1 is a contractual entitlement, not dependent on a separate request.

**Why not D?** § 11.1 specifically provides for this adjustment in a stipulated sum
agreement.

**Cross-reference:** Document A § 11.1, § 5.2; Document B projected dates

### Question 9 — Answer: **C**
**Tag:** CO / H

The Architect's duty is to advise the Owner of the implications of their decisions. If
the Owner insists on adding scope without increasing the budget, Marcus should document
the instruction, quantify the necessary offsets, present options with trade-offs, and
obtain written direction. This protects both the project and the Architect.

**Why not A?** Refusing to work is an overreaction — the Architect's role is to advise,
not to dictate budget decisions.

**Why not B?** Reducing quality without Owner knowledge is a breach of professional
responsibility.

**Why not D?** Pushing the budget problem to the contractor during construction creates
adversarial relationships and may result in unacceptable substitutions.

**Cross-reference:** Document C Email 2; Document A § 5.1

### Question 10 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** WP / H

This answer addresses all converging issues with appropriate urgency and documentation.
The July 15 deadline gives the Owner a clear decision point for the green roof.
StructurePro's claim and the AirFlow claim both need formal processes. The stormwater
ordinance creates a time-sensitivity that should influence the green roof timeline. Meeting
minutes create the paper trail that protects everyone.

**Why not A?** Ignoring the other issues (StructurePro claim, AirFlow claim, stormwater)
is incomplete project management.

**Why not C?** Deferring all decisions wastes the critical window before July 15 and
allows the StructurePro and AirFlow issues to fester.

**Why not D?** Recommending cancellation of the green roof is the Owner's decision,
not the Architect's. The PM should present options and risks.

**Cross-reference:** All documents; Document B schedule notes; Document C Emails 1-5

## Case Study 2: Historic Courthouse Adaptive Reuse

### Question 1 — Answer: **C**
**Tag:** RK / H

Document A § 7.3 explicitly states the Owner acknowledges that visual inspections of
concealed conditions have limitations. The geotechnical/building investigation was an
Owner-furnished service. The concealed termite and rot damage was not visible or
discoverable through the visual inspection that was the agreed-upon scope. The Owner
accepted this risk.

**Why not A?** The Architect's scope was visual inspection only — this was an agreed
limitation, not a failure.

**Why not B?** A concealed condition, by definition, is not something the Contractor
should have anticipated in a historic renovation bid. A201 § 3.7.4 addresses this.

**Why not D?** The structural engineer relied on the investigation scope authorized by
the Owner. Recommending destructive testing was an option, but the Owner limited
investigation to protect the historic fabric (per the narrative).

**Cross-reference:** Document A § 7.3; Document B contractor's position on A201 § 3.7.4

---

### Question 2 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** PE / M

Rachel's memo (Document C-2) identifies three cost components: the contractor's PCO-004
($226,352), Heritage Structural's additional fee ($14,500), and PDA's own additional
services ($13,000-$16,000). All three should be presented to give the Owner a complete
picture. The total impact is $253,852-$256,852, which nearly exhausts the $280,000
contingency.

**Why not A?** This omits the Architect's and consultant's additional fees, understating
the true impact.

**Why not C?** An arbitrary 20% contingency on top of the estimate is not how change
orders are documented — actual costs should be itemized.

**Why not D?** Contractor overhead and profit on changed work is standard per AIA A201.
Trying to strip it creates a dispute.

**Cross-reference:** Document B PCO-004; Document C-2 internal memo

---

### Question 3 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** CO / M

Under AIA A201-2017, a Construction Change Directive (CCD) is a written order signed by
the Owner and Architect directing a change in the Work. It can be issued without the
Contractor's agreement on cost — the cost adjustment is resolved subsequently. This
allows critical work to proceed while negotiations continue.

**Why not A?** That describes a Change Order, not a CCD.

**Why not C?** A Change Order requires agreement from all three parties; a CCD only
requires Owner and Architect signatures.

**Why not D?** CCDs can be used for additions, deletions, or modifications to the work.

**Cross-reference:** AIA A201-2017 § 7.3 (Construction Change Directives)

### Question 4 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** WP / H

The HPC amendment applies only to the 12 windows that need replacement, not the 35
restorable windows. If the October hearing is missed, the approved restoration work on
the 35 windows can proceed, but the subcontractor would need to demobilize and return
later for the 12 replacements — adding mobilization costs and potentially 4+ weeks of
delay to the window scope.

**Why not A?** Only the 12 replacement windows need HPC approval; the 35 restorable
windows are already approved.

**Why not C?** HPC approval for window replacements does not affect National Register
listing.

**Why not D?** Tax credits are related to SHPO review, not to HPC window approvals.

**Cross-reference:** Document C-2 internal memo, Item 2

Losing this creates a funding gap that threatens the project's ability to proceed within

**Why not A?** HPC and SHPO are separate bodies with different jurisdictions.

**Why not B?** Interior modifications don't affect National Register status.

**Why not D?** The capital bond is independent of tax credit compliance.

**Cross-reference:** Document C-1 SHPO letter; scenario narrative (funding structure)

### Question 6 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** PE / H

The structural remediation already requires the second floor to be shored, cleared, and
reworked. Performing the SHPO-required redesign simultaneously means one disruption
instead of two, one set of revised drawings instead of two, and coordinated construction
instead of sequential rework. This is the primary benefit.

**Why not A?** Heritage Structural is still needed for the structural work.

**Why not C?** Billing strategy is a secondary concern — the primary benefit is efficiency.

**Why not D?** The 4-week delay from structural remediation still applies; the benefit is
avoiding *additional* delay from a separate redesign.

**Cross-reference:** Document C-2 internal memo, Recommendation

### Question 7 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** CO / M

Heritage Structural is PDA's subconsultant. PDA holds the contract with the Owner. The
correct flow is: amend the subconsultant agreement, then pass the cost through to the
Owner as Additional Services under § 3.2 (redesign necessitated by unforeseen concealed
conditions is designated as Additional Services in the contract).

**Why not A?** PDA should not absorb costs caused by conditions that are the Owner's
risk.

**Why not C?** Heritage Structural's contract is with PDA, not with the Contractor.
The $14,500 in PCO-004 is the Contractor's estimate for structural engineering — the
actual payment flows through PDA's subconsultant agreement.

**Why not D?** The $64,000 fixed fee covers the original scope. You cannot reduce
committed scope to absorb new work.

**Cross-reference:** Document A § 3.2, § 5.2; Document B PCO-004; Document C-2

### Question 8 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** RM / M

Different tasks require different expertise levels. The Principal handles
preservation-sensitive decisions (appropriate for the SHPO-related redesign), the PM
coordinates, the Project Architect produces revised drawings, and the Technician documents
field conditions. This yields the $13,000-$16,000 estimate at blended rates — efficient
use of the team's skills.

**Why not A?** Assigning all hours to the Principal is inefficient and inflates the cost
beyond what the task requires.

**Why not C?** A Technician cannot make preservation design decisions or coordinate
consultants.

**Why not D?** Section 3.2 designates redesign for unforeseen conditions as Additional
Services, not part of CA basic services.

**Cross-reference:** Document A § 3.2, § 5.2; Document C-2

### Question 9 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** RK / M

The Architect has a professional obligation to advise the Owner of material risks. A
written advisory documenting the SHPO's concerns, the specific financial risk ($2.4M),
and requesting written acknowledgment protects both the project and the Architect. If the
Owner proceeds against advice, the Architect has documented their duty of care.

**Why not A?** Failing to document risk advice is a professional liability exposure.

**Why not C?** Refusing to continue is disproportionate — the Architect advises; the
Owner decides.

**Why not D?** Going around the Owner to the SHPO violates the Architect's duty to
the client. The SHPO will see the final design regardless.

**Cross-reference:** Document C-1 SHPO letter; Document A § 2.6

### Question 10 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** WP / H

The HPC submission has the most urgent deadline (4 days). The structural remediation and
SHPO redesign are interconnected and should be presented as a combined package to avoid
double-work on the second floor. This sequencing addresses all three issues with the
correct priority order.

**Why not A?** Ignoring the HPC deadline (4 days away) while resolving the SHPO issue
first causes a guaranteed 4-week window delay.

**Why not C?** Issuing the CCD for structural work without addressing the SHPO redesign
means potentially reworking the second floor twice.

**Why not D?** A 90-day pause is excessive, costly, and unnecessary when the issues are
manageable with proper sequencing.

**Cross-reference:** All documents; Document C-2 timeline analysis

## Case Study 3: Municipal Library Branch

### Question 1 — Answer: **C**
**Tag:** CO / E

When all bids exceed the budget in a public procurement process, the typical approach is
to negotiate value engineering with the lowest responsible bidder. Redesigning the entire
project is a disproportionate response when the overage can be addressed through targeted
scope adjustments, as was done here.

**Why not A?** Rejecting all bids and redesigning is costly and time-consuming; VE
negotiation is standard practice.

**Why not B?** Public agencies have budget authority limits — they cannot simply accept
a bid that exceeds the approved budget without additional appropriation or scope reduction.

**Why not D?** Negotiating with a non-low bidder undermines the competitive bid process
and may violate procurement law.

**Cross-reference:** Document A, Bid Tabulation and VE Log

---

### Question 2 — Answer: **C**
**Tag:** RK / M

The risk is not just the $11,000 or $42,000 to fix the products — it's the policy
compliance issue. The City mandates LEED Silver for all municipal buildings over 5,000 SF.
Failing to achieve Silver could create political, legal, and reputational issues beyond
the direct remediation costs.

**Why not A/B?** These are remediation costs, not the full risk exposure.

**Why not D?** LEED Silver is mandated by City policy, making it effectively mandatory
for this project.

**Cross-reference:** Document C (LEED scorecard + EcoMetrics memo); scenario narrative
(LEED Silver mandate)

---

### Question 3 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** PE / M

The contractor's concerns are legitimate — long-lead submittals (casework, shelving) do
have real procurement consequences. The professional response is to acknowledge the issue,
prioritize critical items, provide a realistic schedule, and also document any submittals
that were submitted late or incompletely by the contractor (to maintain a balanced record).

**Why not A?** Dismissing a legitimate concern damages the professional relationship and
may create liability if the delay causes real schedule impact.

**Why not C?** Requesting a schedule extension is premature — the backlog may be
clearable within the contractor's timeframes if prioritized properly.

**Why not D?** Billing the Owner for additional staff to address a submittal backlog
would require justification and may not be an Additional Service.

**Cross-reference:** Document B, Contractor letter; contract 10-business-day requirement

---

### Question 4 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** WP / M

The casework has a 12-week lead time from approval. If approved November 17, delivery
would be around February 9. If approved November 28 (11 calendar days later, roughly
8-9 business days), delivery shifts approximately 2 weeks to around February 23,
delaying installation by the same duration.

**Why not A?** Expediting is possible but not guaranteed and typically costs extra.

**Why not C?** The lead time doesn't restart — it begins from the approval date.

**Why not D?** The calculation is straightforward: delay in approval = approximately
equal delay in delivery.

**Cross-reference:** Document B submittal log, contractor letter

### Question 5 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** CO / H

This is a publicly bid contract. The City's procurement code requires City Council
approval for any change order exceeding $25,000. The funding source does not change the
procurement authority requirement — the contract is between the City and the Contractor,
and any modification must follow the same approval process regardless of which municipal
account pays for it.

**Why not A?** The Library Director cannot independently modify a City contract that
went through competitive sealed bidding.

**Why not C?** The item was formally eliminated through VE. Adding it back is a change
order, not a field modification.

**Why not D?** Rebidding is not required for owner-directed additions to an existing
contract (within limits set by procurement law).

**Cross-reference:** Scenario narrative ($25,000 City Council threshold); Document A
VE-05

### Question 6 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** RK / H

Option 2 achieves LEED compliance at $11,000 vs. $42,000 for Option 1, while maintaining
the VE cost structure. It requires contractor cooperation (new submittals) and resubmittal
review time, but the schedule allows for this if acted upon promptly. The roofing membrane
has a 4-week lead time with January 12 installation — there is still time to resolve this.

**Why not A?** Option 1 works but costs nearly 4x more for the same outcome.

**Why not C?** The scorecard shows the project at 53 projected points. Losing 4 drops
it to 49 — below Silver (50). Finding 4 replacement points at this stage is extremely
difficult; most categories are already near their targets.

**Why not D?** The 45 achieved + 8 pending = 53, minus 4 = 49. The margin is too thin
to ignore.

**Cross-reference:** Document C LEED scorecard and EcoMetrics memo

### Question 7 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** PE / E

The submittals were returned as "Revise & Resubmit" because the products match the VE
specification (which removed the EPD requirement) but the Architect recognized that
installing non-EPD products would drop the LEED EPD-certified product count below the
20-product threshold needed for Materials & Resources credits.

**Why not A?** Both VE products still meet fire rating and performance requirements.

**Why not C?** The products are what the VE specification calls for — they're "correct"
per VE but problematic for LEED.

**Why not D?** The submittals themselves were complete; the issue is the products' LEED
compliance characteristics.

**Cross-reference:** Document A VE-01, VE-02; Document C EcoMetrics memo

### Question 8 — Answer: **C**
**Tag:** WP / H

Installation starts January 12, 2026. With a 4-week lead time, the order must be placed
by approximately December 15, 2025. Allowing a few days for processing after approval,
December 15 is the latest practical approval date.

**Why not A?** November 14 would provide more margin but is not the *latest* date.

**Why not B?** December 1 provides 6 weeks, which is more margin than needed.

**Why not D?** January 1 leaves only 11 calendar days — less than the 4-week lead time.

### Question 9 — Answer: **D**
**Tag:** CO / M

The $85,000 for the book return lane comes from the Library Board's operating budget, not
from the bond-funded construction budget. The contingency is part of the bond budget.
Therefore, only the $11,000 LEED fix comes from the construction contingency, leaving
$164,000. The book return lane, while it requires City Council approval (Question 5),
is funded separately and does not reduce the construction contingency.

**Why not A?** This incorrectly subtracts the $85,000 from the bond contingency.

**Why not B?** This ignores both the LEED fix and the book return lane.

**Why not C?** This correctly deducts the $11,000 but the reasoning for excluding the
$85,000 is the key point — which makes D the most complete answer.

**Cross-reference:** Scenario narrative (funding sources); Document A VE log; Document C

### Question 10 — Answer: **B**
**Tag:** RM / H

With a small team (3 people) and multiple competing demands, the PM must prioritize by
impact and delegate by expertise. Long-lead submittals (casework, shelving) get reviewed
first because delays are irreversible. Elena and consultants can handle discipline-specific
submittals (MEP submittals to ClearPoint, structural to Atlas). James personally manages
the higher-level issues (LEED resolution, book return scope) that require Owner/contractor
coordination.

**Why not A?** One person reviewing all 18 submittals creates a bottleneck and delays
the most critical items.

**Why not C?** Requesting additional staff is disproportionate — the backlog is
manageable with proper delegation and prioritization.

**Why not D?** Asking the contractor to delay resubmission worsens the problem.

**Cross-reference:** Document B (submittal log, contractor letter); Document C
(LEED timeline)
