## 4. Distractor Quality (Feedback: "can guess without understanding")

Key improvements:
- Q1 option D: Requires knowledge that you cannot switch procurement methods mid-process. Plausible to someone unfamiliar with public procurement law.
- Q2 option C: Requires knowledge that LEED Certified does not satisfy the City's Silver mandate. Someone unfamiliar with the policy hierarchy might select this as a reasonable fallback.
- Q3 option B: Involves Owner — a reasonable instinct that is premature. Requires understanding that the Architect should assess facts before escalating.
- Q3 option D: Cites the contractual review period — factually correct but procedurally insufficient as a standalone response.
- Q5 option A: References Construction Change Directives — a real AIA mechanism applied incorrectly to this procurement context.
- Q6 option A: Cites real LEED point availability (23 untargeted E&A points) — sounds data-driven but ignores the practical difficulty of earning those points at this project stage.
- Q6 option D: Proposes a wait-and-see approach that sounds strategic but ignores the procurement deadline.
- Q8 option B: Accounts for resubmittal review period — a legitimate consideration but not what the question asks.
- Q9 option D: Argues that contract administration mechanism determines funding source — plausible reasoning but factually incorrect.
- Q10 option D: Proposes triage by having contractor withdraw submittals — sounds like prioritization but actually delays procurement.
